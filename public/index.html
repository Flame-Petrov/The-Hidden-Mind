The Mechanisms

Ten quiet forces, named and disarmed.

When you're bad at something, you often can't tell that you're bad at it — the skill you'd need to judge your work is the same skill you're missing. So beginners feel certain, while real experts hedge.
Once you know how something turned out, your memory quietly rewrites itself so the outcome feels like it was obvious all along — making you a harsh judge of the past and overconfident about the future.
A vivid, specific case grabs your attention while the dull background number — how common the thing actually is — gets ignored, even when that number is what matters most.
Good things thrill you, then fade into your new normal as you drift back to baseline. The same mechanism also helps you recover from bad events faster than you'd expect.
Bad experiences hit harder and linger longer than good ones of the same size — a survival instinct that now mostly makes you replay criticism and overlook the good.
Once you own something, you value it more — just because it's yours. Losing it feels worse than gaining the same thing felt good, so you cling to what you'd never buy again today.
If examples come to mind easily, you assume the thing is common — so dramatic, well-reported events feel far more likely than quieter risks that are actually more frequent.
You judge an experience by its most intense moment and how it ended — not its average. Two moments end up standing in for the whole thing, and length barely registers.
Work expands to fill the time you give it. Extra time invites overthinking and delay, so a tighter deadline often produces the same result, sooner.
Unfinished tasks stay stuck in your mind and keep nagging, while finished ones get released. Just starting a task creates a pull to complete it.

The voice of the whole book.

When you're bad at something, you often can't tell that you're bad at it — so you feel more confident than you should.
Why it happens

The skill you need to do something well is the same skill you need to judge whether you're doing it well. So if you lack the skill, you also lack the ability to see that you lack it. You're missing the very tool that would reveal the problem.

What to do

Be suspicious of your earliest confidence. If something feels simple, you may just be too new to see what's hard about it. And when an expert sounds uncertain, take them more seriously, not less.
